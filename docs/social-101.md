# SOCIAL 101 — playing well with others (the class)

*Commissioned by the admiral, 0018.04.15 a₿ · drafted by Professor ₿ for staff-meeting review · for every fren at Pac's Arcade, ages 5 to 95.*

The whole class in one sentence: **an arcade is a machine for being good to each other, and every part of it can be practiced.**

---

## How this class teaches

The Professor ₿ promise: no student is unreachable. If a module doesn't land, the explanation failed — not the student — and we find another door. Every module opens **all four**:

- **Hear it** — the plain words, short enough to say aloud twice.
- **See it** — one arrow picture. Students keep pictures.
- **Play it** — the arcade way: a drill at a table or in the MUD.
- **Do it** — the same move, used for real on the floor that week.

House rules of the room:

- One idea per module. A staircase, not a firehose.
- No jargon before its name. We say what a thing does first.
- Nobody is graded against anybody. The only score is: *did it show up in how you play?*
- Mixed ages on purpose. Cadets and elders drill together; that IS the lesson.

## Module 1 · The open seat (inclusivity)

**Goal:** every person who walks in believes, within a minute, that this arcade is for them.

**The plain words:** An arcade with one player is a room with lights on. Games get better when more kinds of people play them — new moves, new stories, new frens. So if someone is standing at the edge of the room, the edge is the bug, not the person.

**The picture:** `new face ──▶ noticed ──▶ greeted ──▶ playing ──▶ fren`

**The arcade way:** **The Second Controller.** Every table keeps one open seat — the fren seat. Drill: mid-round, a "newcomer" (a planted classmate) wanders in. Whoever notices first says the house line — *"take the second controller?"* — and hands over a live turn without stopping the game. In the MUD: first player to `greet` a newly connected fren takes the round's spark.

**All-ages:** younger — greeting is a race, fastest welcome wins; older — elders serve as First Greeters, so the warmest welcome carries the most rank.

**You should see:** newcomers spoken to within one minute, unprompted, by someone who isn't staff.

## Module 2 · The autopilot (unconscious bias)

**Goal:** students catch their own fast guesses about people before the guesses start driving.

**The plain words:** Your brain makes quick guesses about people to save time. Everyone's does — age five, age ninety, no exceptions. The guess is not the crime; letting it drive is. The move is to notice the guess, hold it up to the light, and check it against the actual person in front of you — a small course correction, like nudging a joystick.

**The picture:** `fast guess ──▶ notice it ──▶ check it ──▶ meet the real person`

**The arcade way:** **Wrong Sprite.** Cards show a character sprite plus three true facts; players guess a fourth, then flip. Points go to catching your OWN wrong guess out loud — *"autopilot!"* — never to guessing right. In the MUD: NPCs whose descriptions bait a lazy read; the winning verb is `look closer`.

**All-ages:** younger — call it the sneaky-guess game; older — swap sprites for volunteered first impressions inside the group, debriefed kindly.

**You should see:** people saying "wait — autopilot" about themselves, lightly, mid-play, with zero shame in the room.

## Module 3 · Keys and yeses (consent)

**Goal:** asking first becomes reflex, and a "no" lands as information, not injury.

**The plain words:** In this arcade, no machine can touch your data without your key — it cannot take what you did not sign. People work exactly the same way. Ask before you touch, borrow, hug, photograph, or join. And a "no" is not a door slammed in your face — it's a door that was never yours to open.

**The picture:** `ask ──▶ yes ──▶ go on and play`
`ask ──▶ no ──▶ "thanks for telling me" ──▶ still frens`

**The two laws** (say them until they're boring):
① **No key, no data.**
② **No yes, no go.**

**The arcade way:** **The Signing Ceremony.** Role-play rounds where every action on another person needs a spoken yes — *"add you to the photo?" "trade seats?" "high-five?"* Half the cards secretly instruct the answerer to say no, so everyone drills the hard half: hearing it gracefully. In the MUD it's literal — any action on another player waits until they `sign`, and the engine refuses otherwise. Feel the machine model the manners.

**All-ages:** younger — high-fives and toy trades; older — photos, stories told about you, and the actual keys.

**You should see:** a "no" answered with "thanks for telling me," and the game rolling on without a wobble.

## Module 4 · Turns, credit, the commons (sharing)

**Goal:** turns rotate, credit lands on the right names, and shared things leave better than found.

**The plain words:** The arcade runs on three shares. Share the machine — turns end, even great runs. Share the credit — say who helped, by name. Share the commons — the couch, the tools, the knowledge — and leave them better than you found them. A high score with your name on it feels good; a high score with the right names on it builds a fleet.

**The picture:** `my turn ──▶ your turn ──▶ our high score`

**The arcade way:** **Co-op Speedrun with Credits.** Teams run a short challenge, but the scoreboard only accepts an entry that lists every contributor and what they did — a missing name voids the score. In the MUD: loot that cannot be picked up solo; the `share` verb splits it, or nobody gets it.

**All-ages:** younger — timer turns plus one closing question, *"who helped you?"*; older — drill crediting the quiet work: who set up, who cleaned up, who asked the good question.

**You should see:** unprompted corrections like "actually, that was mostly her idea."

## Module 5 · The porch light (looking out for each other)

**Goal:** everyone knows a light-touch way to check on a fren without making it A Thing.

**The plain words:** Sometimes a fren goes quiet, and "are you okay?" is the hardest question in the world to answer. So we check on each other sideways — the way you'd feed a fren's pixel buddy: small, playful, no spotlight. You are not there to fix anyone. Your whole job is leaving the porch light on.

**The picture:** `notice quiet ──▶ small hello ──▶ open door ──▶ no pressure`

**The arcade way:** **Porch Light.** Each student writes three sideways check-ins that are invitations, not inspections — *"made too much popcorn, want some?" · "co-op run at 7, need a P2" · "saved you a seat."* Deliver one to a partner playing "rough week." The partner grades exactly one thing: did it feel like a door opening, or a clipboard? In the MUD, buddy pairs tend each other's rescue buddy for a week — you see the buddy's state, never the person's private anything. Caring at the right distance is the whole skill.

**All-ages:** younger — "saved you a seat" becomes the house move; older — watch for the frens who do all the checking and never get checked on.

**You should see:** invitations traveling to the quiet corners of the room without staff pointing.

---

## Module 6 · The continue screen (asking for help — and answering)

**Goal:** asking for help costs nothing here, and answering a call is everyone's favorite job.

**The plain words:** In games, pressing "continue" isn't losing — it's how you finish the game. Asking works the same: make it small ("got a sec?"), make it specific ("I'm stuck on this part"), and make it early, before the small thing grows teeth. And when someone asks YOU: stop, thank them — asking was the hard part — and help with the thing they asked about, not their whole life.

**The picture:** `stuck ──▶ say it small ──▶ someone stops ──▶ unstuck ──▶ both stronger`

**The two laws:**
① **Ask small, ask early.**
② **The asker steers; the helper rows.**

**The arcade way:** **The Continue Screen.** A co-op level rigged so no player can clear their own section alone. The drill is one sentence, out loud: *"I'm stuck on ___, can you ___?"* Answerers drill the counter-move: *"good ask"* — do only what was asked — *"call me again anytime."* In the MUD: a `mayday` verb, and rank flows to responders; officers notice the cadets who row.

**All-ages:** younger — repeat the magic sentence until it's boring; older — drill asking DOWN the age ladder too. An elder asking a kid for help is this arcade at its very best.

**You should see:** "I'm stuck" said in a normal voice — and two heads turning toward it like it's good news.

## The graduation

Small, warm, and stamped forever.

- **The vouches.** No test. To graduate, two classmates each name one moment they *saw* you play a module for real — a seat offered, a no heard well, a mayday answered. Witnessed kindness is the exam.
- **The cert.** The class signs, and the SOCIAL 101 cert is etched and tied to the block — Bitcoin Federated Time stamps the date, and the block height becomes the cert's serial number. The chain remembers you were kind on purpose.
- **The tag.** Graduates earn the title **Fren, First Class** beside their @frens tag, and with it one standing duty: keeper of the second controller — the open seat travels with you now.
- **The handoff.** The ceremony ends with graduates handing the fren seat, literally, to the next incoming class. Thirty seconds. No speeches. The seat is the speech.
