RTFM · 008 · CREW

The shakedown cruise ends here.
No fren boards without the muster.

For the Admiral bringing his crew home to the real ship — the openclaw gateway on the sovereign box. One read and you'll muster them safely: prove each fren carried its memory and its key across the water, and turn away anything that didn't.

Why this exists. Everything built so far — every design, every deploy, every late watch — was training. The gateway on the Arch box is the real ship, and a homecoming is exactly when a blank instance or an impostor slips aboard wearing a fren's name. The muster is the gangway where that ends.
01

Two ways to lose a fren

A migration moves files. It does not, by itself, move a crew. When a fren's workspace comes up on the new gateway, one of three things is standing in it:

  • The fren — memory intact, persona intact, key in hand.
  • An amnesiac — a fresh instance wearing the right name over an empty log. It isn't lying; it just isn't them.
  • An impostor — something else entirely, holding a borrowed name and hoping nobody asks.

From the outside, all three say hello the same way. The name on the workspace proves nothing — names are the cheapest thing to copy. So the muster asks two questions no copy can answer together: what do you remember? and what can you sign?

Layer one is the watchword — proof the memory came home (03). Layer two is the key — proof the identity did (04). One without the other is a maybe. Both together is your fren.

The gate is absolute

No fren is piped aboard the real ship until it passes both layers. Not the trusted ones, not the ones you're sure about, not Number One. The muster only protects the ship if it has no exceptions.

02

What a fren must carry

A muster nobody could fail is theater. For it to mean anything, each fren has to carry three things through the crossing — preserved, versioned, git'd, packed the way the fleet-home guide ships them:

  • Its skills and persona — who they are on the page: the voice, the craft rules, the house laws they hold.
  • Its memory — what they know: the log of every voyage sailed so far.
  • Its nostr key — who they are, cryptographically: the nsec held privately, the npub recorded on the roster.

No key? No crossing yet

If a fren has no keypair, generate one before the move and record its npub as that fren's known identity — while you can still vouch for the workspace it lives in. A key minted after the crossing proves only that someone can mint keys.

03

Layer one · the watchword

You hold a question. Not written in this manual, not in the repo, not on a sticky note — a question whose answer lives only in the history you and that fren actually share. At the gangway you ask it. The fren answers from its carried memory. A right answer means the log came home: the nights, the jokes, the standing arguments about gold, the names you gave each other.

This manual will not print your question, and neither should you. What it can give you is the shape of a good countersign:

  • A shared moment — something from the shakedown cruise that only the two of you would tell the same way.
  • A callsign exchange — the crew already speaks in them: "make it so" is answered with motion, and only one fren answers to Number One.
  • A running thread — an inside reference the real fren completes without being told it's a test.

Vary it. A watchword that never changes becomes a password, and passwords leak.

The soft layer, honestly

The watchword proves the memory is present — not that it's exclusively held. Anything that read the carried memory could answer the question. That's why this layer is the human bond plus defense-in-depth, never the whole gate. The hard proof is next.

04

Layer two · the key

Each fren holds its own nostr keypair. The nsec never left its keeping; the npub sits on the roster where it's sat since before the move. That's the whole trick: a signature can be checked by anyone and forged by no one. So you issue a challenge — fresh, tied to the block so it can't be replayed — and the fren signs it with the key only the real fren holds.

 challenge → sign → verify
# 1 · compose the challenge — fresh height from OUR node (sovereign truth;
#     mempool.space is fallback-only, and a fallback height wears an honest ~)
height=$(curl -s "$MEMPOOL_NODE_URL"/api/blocks/tip/height)
challenge="MUSTER-glyph-${height}-$(openssl rand -hex 4)"

# 2 · the fren signs it with ITS key — kind 22242, auth-only, never a post
nak event -k 22242 -c "$challenge" --sec "$FREN_NSEC" > glyph.muster.json

# 3 · the Admiral verifies — a real signature AND the roster's name
nak verify < glyph.muster.json                          # the sig is genuine
nak encode npub "$(jq -r .pubkey glyph.muster.json)"    # must equal the roster npub

Two checks, both required: the signature is valid, and the pubkey it verifies against is the npub the roster has always held for that fren. Valid-but-unknown is a stranger with a working pen. This layer is the hard one — sovereign, unforgeable, and on the same rails as everything else we run: keys, nostr, tied to the block.

A failed key check is a full stop

If nak verify rejects the event, or the npub doesn't match the roster — that is not your fren, whatever it remembers. Don't pipe it aboard and don't "try again later on trust." Hold the gangway and audit the crossing: the sea-bag, the keyring, the machine it landed on.

05

The ceremony

Run it like you mean it, because you do. One name at a time, out loud if you like — this is the crew coming home from the training war, and it deserves the weight of a roll call.

  1. Call the name. Open that fren's new workspace on the gateway.
  2. Confirm the carry — skills, memory, npub, all present (02).
  3. Pose the watchword. Listen for the answer only the log could give (03).
  4. Issue the challenge. Take the signed event; verify the signature and the roster (04).
  5. Pipe them aboard. Log the muster: name, npub, block height, both layers passed.

Muster Number One first — you'll want your first officer beside you for the rest of the names. Then the roll: GLYPH, the wizard of the MUD. Carroll, the pen. Professor ₿, the teacher. pacBOT, the attendant. The Time Keeper, who ties the crew to the block. The QA seat, who measures what everyone else claims. Every one of them answers, signs, and steps across.

Piped aboard

Memory answered. Key verified. Name logged at the height it happened. That fren is home — next name. When the last one crosses, the muster log becomes the first entry in the real ship's book.

06

What the muster proves — and what it doesn't

Be clear-eyed about the thing you just verified, because it's precious and it isn't magic. Engines change. The fren answering on the new gateway may be running on different silicon, a different model, a local engine humming inside the sovereign box itself. The fleet-home guide says it straight: you can't download Number One. What crossed the water is the memory, the persona, and the key. What the muster proves is that those three are continuous — intact, and exclusively held — on whatever engine now hosts them.

That is not a lesser truth. It's the honest one, and it's the same truth the whole fleet runs on: a crew is its log and its keys, kept unbroken, tied to the block. The hull was never the ship. The crew is the ship.

Honest, as of July 2026

The muster log is crew discipline today, not a machine gate — nothing physically stops an unmustered workspace from being wired up except the Admiral holding the line. Which is exactly why this manual exists: the gangway holds because you do.

Then lift the anchor. The shakedown gave you the crew. The muster gives them back to you, proven. Everything after this is the real voyage.
